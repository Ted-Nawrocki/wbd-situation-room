On February 27, 2026, Netflix co-CEOs Ted Sarandos and Greg Peters issued a short statement. They were "declining to match" Paramount Skydance's revised offer. With that, a two-month bidding war ended. Warner Bros. Discovery and Paramount Skydance signed a definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger the same day.

The contest is over. What follows is the closing process.

## Just the facts

**The winning transaction:**
- Paramount Skydance to acquire all outstanding WBD shares at $31.00 per share in all cash
- Enterprise value: approximately $110.9 billion
- Covers the entire WBD enterprise, no separation required
- Merger Agreement end date: March 4, 2027, subject to extension
- Paramount reverse termination fee: $5.8 billion if the deal fails to close for regulatory reasons

**The February 26-27 sequence:**
- February 26: After receiving a contractual waiver from Netflix, WBD's board reopened negotiations with Paramount
- February 26: The board unanimously determined that Paramount's revised $31.00 per share offer constituted a Superior Proposal under the Netflix merger agreement
- February 26: WBD notified Netflix of the determination, triggering Netflix's contractual matching rights period
- February 27: Netflix co-CEOs announced Netflix would not raise its offer
- February 27: WBD terminated its merger agreement with Netflix, triggering the $2.8 billion termination fee, funded by Paramount
- February 27: WBD and Paramount Skydance signed the definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger

**The final offers compared:**
- Paramount: $31.00 per share, all cash, entire enterprise, no separation required
- Netflix (withdrawn): $27.75 per share, all cash, Streaming and Studios only, separation required

**Financing:**
- Cash on hand and committed debt financing; equity and debt syndications confirmed complete at the April 2026 shareholder vote

## The path that got here

Paramount launched its hostile tender at $30.00 per share on December 8, 2025. The WBD board rejected it twice while reaffirming the Netflix transaction. On January 20, 2026, Netflix converted its offer to all-cash at $27.75, removing the structural critique that had been Paramount's primary public argument.

On February 10, 2026, Paramount revised its offer and extended the tender again. The WBD board rejected that version too, with Di Piazza stating the offer remained "inferior to our merger agreement with Netflix across multiple key areas."

What changed between February 10 and February 26 was one dollar. Paramount moved to $31.00. That $3.25 premium over Netflix's $27.75, applied to the whole company with no separation required, created a gap the board could not responsibly ignore.

## Ted's Takes

The Netflix waiver is the detail most coverage skipped over. WBD was contractually barred from engaging with Paramount without Netflix's permission. Netflix chose to grant it. That decision reflects a clear-eyed financial judgment: at $31.00 for the whole enterprise, matching Paramount would have required Netflix to pay for Global Networks, a business it had no plan for. Granting the waiver, collecting the $2.8 billion termination fee, and preserving capital was the correct call. Netflix did not lose this process. It chose not to win at that price.

Paramount's move from $30.00 to $31.00 was calibrated. One dollar was enough to give the WBD board the basis for a different fiduciary conclusion without dramatically overpaying. The board had held its position through multiple rejections with disciplined public language. When it changed course, the market believed it because the reasoning was consistent throughout. WBD shareholders ended up with $31.00 in cash for the whole company, no separation required, versus $27.75 plus Discovery Global shares through a multi-step process. That is a materially better outcome.
