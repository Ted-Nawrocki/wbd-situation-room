On February 27, 2026, Netflix co-CEOs Ted Sarandos and Greg Peters issued a short statement. They were "declining to match" Paramount Skydance's revised offer. With those two words, a two-month bidding war ended. Warner Bros. Discovery and Paramount Skydance signed a definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger the same day.

The contest is over. What follows is the closing process.

## Just the facts

**The winning transaction:**
- Paramount Skydance to acquire all outstanding WBD shares at $31.00 per share in all cash
- Enterprise value: approximately $110.9 billion
- Covers the entire WBD enterprise -- no separation required
- Merger Agreement end date: March 4, 2027 (subject to extension)
- Paramount reverse termination fee: $5.8 billion (payable if deal fails to close for regulatory reasons)

**How the February 26-27 sequence unfolded:**
- February 26: After receiving a contractual waiver from Netflix, WBD's board reopened negotiations with Paramount
- February 26: WBD's board unanimously determined that Paramount's revised $31.00/share offer constituted a "Superior Proposal" under the Netflix merger agreement
- February 26: WBD notified Netflix of the Superior Proposal determination, triggering Netflix's contractual matching rights period
- February 27: Netflix co-CEOs announced Netflix would not raise its offer and declined to match
- February 27: WBD terminated its merger agreement with Netflix; the $2.8 billion Netflix termination fee was triggered, funded by Paramount
- February 27: WBD and Paramount Skydance signed the definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger

**The final offer comparison:**
- Paramount: $31.00 per share, all cash, entire enterprise, no separation required
- Netflix (withdrawn): $27.75 per share, all cash, Streaming and Studios only, separation required

**Financing:**
- Paramount intends to fund the transaction through a combination of cash on hand and committed debt financing
- Equity and debt syndications subsequently completed, as confirmed at the April 2026 shareholder vote

## The path that got here

Paramount launched its hostile tender offer at $30.00 per share on December 8, 2025. WBD's board rejected it -- twice -- while reaffirming its commitment to the Netflix transaction. On January 20, 2026, Netflix converted its offer to all-cash at $27.75, removing the structural critique that had been Paramount's primary public argument.

Then, on February 10, 2026, Paramount filed a revised offer letter and extended its tender offer again. WBD's board rejected that version too, with Chairman Samuel Di Piazza stating the offer remained "inferior to our merger agreement with Netflix across multiple key areas" and provided "insufficient value."

What changed between February 10 and February 26 was price. Paramount moved to $31.00 per share -- a $1.00 increase from its prior position and a $3.25 premium over Netflix's $27.75. That gap, combined with the whole-company scope requiring no separation mechanics, gave the WBD board the basis for a different fiduciary conclusion.

## Ted's Takes

Two things happened in the February 26-27 sequence that are easy to miss in the headlines.

The first is the Netflix waiver. Under the terms of the Netflix merger agreement, WBD was contractually prohibited from soliciting or engaging with competing bidders without Netflix's consent. The "no-shop" provision is standard in negotiated merger agreements. For WBD to reopen negotiations with Paramount, Netflix had to affirmatively grant permission. Netflix chose to do so.

That is not a passive or automatic event. Netflix management had to make a deliberate decision to allow the board to engage. The most plausible explanation is that Netflix had already done the analysis: at $31.00 for the entire enterprise -- including the Global Networks linear business that Netflix had no strategic use for -- matching Paramount would have required Netflix to either accept a materially diluted return on the studio assets or assign significant value to a business it was not acquiring. Neither outcome was financially defensible. Granting the waiver, collecting the $2.8 billion termination fee, and preserving capital for other uses was the correct call.

The second thing worth noting is the precision of Paramount's final price. The move from $30.00 to $31.00 is one dollar. It was not a dramatic escalation. But it was calibrated to accomplish something specific: create enough of a gap that the WBD board could no longer credibly argue the Netflix and Paramount offers were comparable on economic terms. At $30.00 versus $27.75, the premium was $2.25 and the structural argument about scope and certainty could carry significant weight. At $31.00 versus $27.75, the premium is $3.25 for the whole company with no separation required. The board's fiduciary obligation to evaluate that gap becomes harder to discharge without engagement.

Paramount had been at $30.00 since December. The timing of the $31.00 move -- arriving in mid-February after multiple rejections -- suggests Paramount had been holding that increment in reserve and deployed it when it judged the board's position to be most open to reconsideration. That is patient, disciplined bidding.

Netflix's exit language deserves attention. "Declining to match" is precise. It frames the decision as a choice, not a concession. Netflix did not lose this contest in any meaningful sense -- it chose not to compete at a price that would have impaired returns for its own shareholders. The co-CEOs' joint statement was brief and unapologetic. There was no language about disappointment, no suggestion that the outcome was anything other than a rational financial decision. That communication posture matters. Netflix's investors needed to hear that management walked away from a deal that no longer made sense, not that management failed to win a bidding war.

For WBD shareholders, the final economics are significantly better than what was available in December. The original Netflix offer implied $27.75 in cash plus Discovery Global shares, with the uncertainty of a multi-step separation process built into the timeline. The Paramount transaction delivers $31.00 in cash for the entire company, with no separation required and a cleaner path to closing. The $3.25 per share improvement over the Netflix price, applied across the WBD share count, represents a material aggregate value increase.

The $2.8 billion Netflix termination fee funded by Paramount is worth pausing on. Paramount absorbed a $2.8 billion cost as part of winning this process. That sum is not trivial -- it is approximately 2.5% of the total deal value. It flows directly from WBD to Netflix, compensating Netflix for the value of the merger agreement it surrendered. From Paramount's perspective, it was a known cost of the endgame and was presumably incorporated into its financial analysis of the transaction from early in the process.

From a corporate actions standpoint, the February 27 signing date is the moment the operational playbook changes entirely. The relevant documents from this point are the Paramount merger agreement, the Paramount Schedule TO and its amendments, and the debt management filings that follow. The Netflix proxy materials, the 14D-9 amendments, the Delaware litigation -- those are now history. The processing framework is a pending acquisition awaiting regulatory clearance.
