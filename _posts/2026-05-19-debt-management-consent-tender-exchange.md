With the shareholder vote behind them and regulatory approval ahead, Paramount Skydance and Warner Bros. Discovery launched three concurrent debt management operations on May 19, 2026. All three are linked. All three are conditional on the acquisition closing. And for operations teams holding any of the affected instruments, two of the three have deadlines measured in days, not months.

This is the part of a major acquisition that never makes the front page. It is also the part where process failures are most expensive.

## Just the facts

**WBD consent solicitations:**
- Issuers: Discovery Global Holdings, Inc. (DGH, formerly WarnerMedia Holdings, Inc.) and Discovery Communications, LLC (DCL)
- Scope: 17 series of outstanding notes; interest rates 3.950% to 5.300%; maturities 2027 to 2052
- Purpose: amend indentures to extend the deadline for Required Exchange Transactions from December 30, 2026 to the Merger Agreement end date of March 4, 2027, subject to extension; additional amendments address terms of any Junior Lien Exchange Notes depending on whether the acquisition closes
- Consent threshold: majority in aggregate principal amount per indenture
- Consent deadline: May 26, 2026 at 5:00 p.m. New York time
- Consent payment: $2.50 per $1,000 principal amount (EUR 2.50 per EUR 1,000 for euro-denominated series), funded by Paramount
- Payment expected: on or about May 29, 2026
- Solicitation agents: BofA Securities and Citigroup
- Tabulation and information agent: Global Bondholder Services Corporation

**Paramount cash tender offers:**
- Issuer of target notes: DGH and DCL
- Target series (two only):
  - 3.755% Senior Notes due 2027 (DGH Issuer): $1,195,271,000 outstanding (CUSIP 55903V BL6)
  - 3.950% Senior Notes due 2028 (DCL Issuer): $1,249,026,000 outstanding (CUSIP 25470D BS7)
- Aggregate outstanding of tender offer notes: approximately $2.44 billion
- Consideration: cash, calculated at a fixed spread of 0 basis points over the applicable U.S. Treasury benchmark, determined at 10:00 a.m. New York time on the expiration date
- Accrued interest: paid in cash through but not including the settlement date
- Expiration: June 17, 2026 at 5:00 p.m. New York time
- Settlement: on or shortly after the acquisition closing date, targeted Q3 2026
- Accepted notes: cancelled and retired; no longer outstanding obligations of the WBD issuers
- Structure: any-and-all; no proration mechanism

**Paramount exchange offers:**
- Issuer of target notes: DGH and DCL
- Target series: thirteen longer-dated series, maturities 2029 to 2052, USD and euro denominated
- Aggregate outstanding: approximately $12.1 billion USD-denominated plus approximately EUR 574 million euro-denominated
- Consideration: newly issued Paramount Skydance second-lien secured notes (New PSKY Notes) with matching currency, maturity dates, interest payment dates, and interest rates, with certain exceptions as specified in the offering memorandum
- Exchange ratio: $1,000 (or EUR 1,000) principal amount of New PSKY Notes per $1,000 (or EUR 1,000) principal amount of existing notes
- No cash payment or premium in the exchange offers
- Expiration: June 17, 2026 at 5:00 p.m. New York time
- Settlement: on or shortly after the acquisition closing date, targeted Q3 2026
- Accepted notes: retired and cancelled; not reissued
- Structure: any-and-all; no proration mechanism
- Eligibility restriction: exchange offers are available only to qualified institutional buyers (Rule 144A) or non-U.S. persons (Regulation S); non-QIB domestic holders are not eligible

**Conditions applying to both offers:**
- Requisite consents received in the WBD consent solicitations
- Consummation of the acquisition
- Participation in either offer requires prior delivery of consents in the WBD consent solicitations
- Upon delivering consents, holders receive a temporary CUSIP or ISIN (temporary identifier) for their applicable notes; only notes bearing a temporary identifier are eligible to participate in the applicable offer

**Dealer managers (both offers):** BofA Securities and Citigroup  
**Exchange agent and information agent:** Global Bondholder Services Corporation  
**Offer documents and eligibility certification:** gbsc-usa.com/paramount  
**Legal counsel to Paramount:** Latham and Watkins LLP  
**Legal counsel to dealer managers:** Cahill Gordon and Reindel LLP

## Broader financing context

Paramount has disclosed plans to replace its $49 billion acquisition bridge loan with approximately $39.5 billion of first-lien secured debt and $12.4 billion of second-lien secured debt, subject to market conditions. The New PSKY Notes issued in the exchange offers will form part of the second-lien secured layer. Paramount has committed to ratings agencies to delever to below 3.75x net debt to adjusted EBITDA by 2028 and 3.0x by 2029. Paramount has also disclosed to eligible holders a target of over $6 billion in combined cost and revenue synergies from the Paramount-WBD-Skydance platform, with approximately 30% expected within the first year post-close, 70% by year two, and full run-rate by year three.

## Ted's Takes

Three operations launched on the same day, all conditional on the same acquisition, all requiring action on different timelines. Let me work through each layer.

**The consent solicitation: a deadline that gates everything else**

On its face, the WBD consent solicitation is a scheduling amendment. The existing WBD indentures require the company to launch a Junior Lien Exchange offer by December 30, 2026. If the Paramount acquisition closes on the Q3 2026 target timeline, that obligation becomes irrelevant -- the combined company's capital structure will look nothing like what the indentures anticipated. But if the acquisition encounters delays and closes in late 2026 or early 2027, the December 30 deadline becomes a live obligation that could force the combined entity to execute an exchange it neither needs nor wants. Extending that deadline to March 4, 2027 -- the Merger Agreement end date -- eliminates the problem.

The $2.50 consent fee is an incentive, not compensation for economic concession. Holders are not being asked to give up anything of substance. The amendments do not change rates, maturities, or principal amounts. For institutional holders who have already decided to support the acquisition, delivering consent is a formality that happens to pay $2.50 per $1,000. The five-day window between launch and deadline is short but achievable for organized operations teams.

What makes the consent solicitation operationally significant is its position in the dependency chain. Holders cannot participate in the Paramount tender or exchange offers without first delivering consent. Miss the May 26 deadline and the gate to both offers closes. For holders of exchange offer notes who want new PSKY paper rather than holding WBD subsidiary obligations to maturity, that sequencing deserves attention.

**Why the tender and exchange offers target different series**

The structural split between the two short-dated tender offer series and the thirteen longer-dated exchange offer series is deliberate and worth understanding precisely.

The 2027 and 2028 maturities are being retired for cash. They mature so soon that replacing them with newly issued Paramount second-lien notes with matching long-dated maturities would create an immediate mismatch. There is no commercial logic to exchanging a note that matures in 12 to 24 months for a new instrument that runs to 2029 or beyond. Cash retirement is the right answer: Paramount pays par plus accrued interest, the instruments are cancelled, and the WBD issuer subsidiaries' obligations on those two series are extinguished at closing.

The thirteen longer-dated series -- 2029 through 2052 -- are being exchanged because they have enough remaining life to make the obligor substitution commercially rational for both sides. Paramount gets to integrate these instruments into its permanent capital structure without a cash outlay. Holders get continuity of economic terms -- same coupon, same maturity, same currency -- with a change in obligor and security package.

That last point is where holders need to form an independent view. The exchange is a credit substitution. The obligor changes from a WBD subsidiary (Discovery Global Holdings or Discovery Communications) to Paramount Skydance Corporation. The security package changes from unsecured senior obligations of WBD subsidiaries to second-lien secured obligations of the combined company. The combined company will carry substantially more leverage than the WBD subsidiary obligors do today, reflecting the acquisition financing. The coupon is preserved -- but the entity behind that coupon is different, and the capital structure above the second lien is substantial. Holders who exchange are making a credit judgment about the combined Paramount-WBD entity, not just accepting a mechanical rebooking.

**No proration, but an eligibility screen that matters**

Both sets of offers are structured as any-and-all. The caps -- $2.4 billion for the tender, $12.8 billion for the exchange -- are sized by reference to the total outstanding principal of each respective pool, not set below it. Under full participation, both pools are accommodated without proration.

The eligibility restriction on the exchange offers is a different kind of constraint, and one that operations teams cannot afford to overlook. Only QIBs and non-U.S. persons under Regulation S may participate. The exchange offers are not registered under the Securities Act of 1933. Non-QIB domestic holders are simply ineligible, regardless of whether they deliver consents. A non-QIB holder who delivers consent will receive the $2.50 consent payment, but cannot submit exchange instructions. Their notes remain outstanding WBD subsidiary obligations after closing.

Custodians and intermediaries need to screen their holder populations before submitting instructions. The eligibility certification process runs through GBSC at gbsc-usa.com/paramount. Submitting exchange instructions for a non-eligible holder is not a recoverable error at the point of settlement.

**The temporary identifier: a trading split that requires system attention**

Once a holder delivers consent, their notes are re-identified with a temporary CUSIP or ISIN. From that point until the offer expires, two distinct trading populations exist for each series: consenting holders with the temporary identifier and non-consenting holders retaining the original identifier. Only notes bearing the temporary identifier are eligible to participate in the applicable offer.

This is standard mechanics in consent-linked tender and exchange offer structures, but it is not something every system handles cleanly. Platforms that do not recognize temporary identifiers as linked to the original CUSIP or ISIN will create position fragmentation and reconciliation problems. Operations teams should verify ahead of the consent deadline that their systems can track both identifiers simultaneously and link them correctly through to settlement.

Secondary market trading in the consented notes adds another layer. A holder who delivers consent and then sells the re-identified notes transfers both the temporary identifier and the right to participate in the offer. A holder who buys re-identified notes in the secondary market after the consent deadline cannot revoke the prior consent. Position management during the window between consent delivery and offer expiration requires care.

**The multi-month settlement gap**

Both offers expire June 17. Settlement occurs on or shortly after the acquisition closing date, targeted Q3 2026. That is a gap of potentially several months between the expiration date and the settlement date, during which tendered notes remain outstanding obligations of the WBD issuers, positions sit in a tendered-but-unsettled state, and the settlement clock does not start until regulatory clearance is received.

For custody operations, this is not an unusual structure in acquisition-conditioned offers, but it is one that requires active position management. The WBD issuer subsidiaries continue to make interest payments on tendered notes until the settlement date. Those payments flow to the tendering holders. The new Paramount notes begin accruing interest from the last interest payment date on the exchanged series, and on the first interest payment date after settlement, Paramount pays the combined accrued interest. Systems need to track both the pre-settlement interest on the tendered series and the post-settlement accrual on the new PSKY notes.

The bridge between those two interest streams is clean in the documentation -- but clean documentation and clean systems reconciliation are different things. Operations teams have from now until June 17 to get their platforms aligned.
