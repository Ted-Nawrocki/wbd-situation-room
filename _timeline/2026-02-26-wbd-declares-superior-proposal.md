title: "WBD Board Determines Paramount's Revised $31.00 Offer Is a Superior Proposal"
fact: "On February 26, 2026, after receiving a contractual waiver from Netflix permitting engagement with Paramount, WBD's board of directors reopened negotiations with Paramount Skydance. Following that review, the board unanimously determined that Paramount's revised all-cash offer of $31.00 per share, valuing the entire WBD enterprise at approximately $110.9 billion, constituted a 'Superior Proposal' under the terms of its existing merger agreement with Netflix. The $31.00 per share price represents a $3.25 per share increase over Netflix's offer of $27.75 per share. The Superior Proposal determination triggered a contractual notice period during which Netflix had the right to revise its offer to match or exceed Paramount's terms. Paramount's revised offer covered the entire WBD enterprise -- not just the Streaming and Studios business -- and did not require completion of the Discovery Global separation as a precondition to closing."
context: "The word 'waiver' is doing a lot of work here. Under the terms of the Netflix merger agreement, WBD was contractually prohibited from soliciting or engaging with competing bidders without Netflix's consent. For this to happen, Netflix had to affirmatively grant permission for WBD to talk to Paramount. That is not a passive event. It signals that Netflix's own analysis of Paramount's revised offer had shifted -- not necessarily that Netflix was conceding, but that Netflix recognized the board had a fiduciary obligation to evaluate a materially higher bid.\n\nThe $31.00 figure is also a deliberate escalation. Paramount moved from $30.00 to $31.00, a $1.00 increase, which is enough to create a meaningful spread relative to Netflix's $27.75 but calibrated to avoid bidding far beyond what the underlying assets support. The $3.25 gap gives institutional shareholders a clear economic argument for the switch, even after factoring in execution risk.\n\nThe 'Superior Proposal' determination is a defined term in the merger agreement, not editorial language. By making this determination, the board is formally triggering the contractual matching rights process. Netflix now has a defined window -- typically three to five business days under standard merger agreement terms -- to respond with a revised offer that the board would be required to evaluate. If Netflix matches, the process resets. If it does not, WBD is contractually permitted to terminate the Netflix agreement and enter into the Paramount transaction, subject to payment of the applicable termination fee.\n\nFrom a process standpoint, this is the moment the situation turns. Everything that follows -- Netflix's response, the definitive Paramount agreement, the shareholder vote -- flows directly from this board determination."
