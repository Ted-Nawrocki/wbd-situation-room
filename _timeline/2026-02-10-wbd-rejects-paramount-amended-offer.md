title: "WBD Board Unanimously Rejects Paramount's Revised Offer"
fact: "On February 10, 2026, Paramount Skydance filed an 8-K announcing it had submitted a revised offer letter to the WBD board and had amended and extended its tender offer for all outstanding WBD shares. WBD's board, through Chairman Samuel A. Di Piazza Jr., responded with a unanimous rejection. Di Piazza stated that the board had 'unanimously determined that Paramount's latest offer remains inferior to our merger agreement with Netflix across multiple key areas.' In a letter to shareholders, Di Piazza wrote that Paramount's offer continues to provide 'insufficient value' and carries 'significant costs, risks and uncertainties as compared to the Netflix merger.' He specifically cited a lack of certainty around finalization of the Paramount structure, noting that if the company were to agree to the Paramount merger and it failed to close, it would result in 'potentially considerable value destruction.' WBD CEO David Zaslav told employees in an internal memo that the company's 'operating plans remain unchanged' and that its priorities for 2026 are 'clear and intentional.'"
context: "The board's rejection language is more pointed here than in December. Where earlier statements focused on process, this one focuses on value and certainty. Di Piazza is doing something specific: he is not just saying Paramount's price is insufficient, he is arguing that the structure of Paramount's offer introduces execution risk that the Netflix transaction does not. That framing matters because it shifts the debate from a simple dollar comparison to a more nuanced discussion of deal certainty, regulatory timeline, and what happens to shareholders if Paramount's offer fails to close.\n\nThe 'potentially considerable value destruction' line is the most consequential language in the rejection. It signals that the board has done the analysis on what happens in a termination scenario and has concluded that the downside of a failed Paramount transaction exceeds the premium Paramount is offering. That is a specific fiduciary judgment, one that Paramount will need to directly rebut rather than simply outbid.\n\nFor corporate actions practitioners, the sequencing here is operationally significant. Every extension of the Paramount tender offer requires new notice periods, amended Schedule TO filings, and updated communications to holders. The accumulated administrative weight of multiple extensions also tells a story: Paramount has now extended this offer multiple times without securing board support, which creates questions about how much further it can reasonably extend before the credibility of the offer itself is impaired."
