title: "Netflix Declines to Match; WBD Signs Definitive Agreement with Paramount at $31.00 per Share"
fact: "On February 27, 2026, Netflix co-CEOs Ted Sarandos and Greg Peters announced that Netflix would not raise its offer for Warner Bros. Discovery, declining to match Paramount Skydance's $31.00 per share bid. The co-CEOs stated they were 'declining to match' Paramount's offer after determining the deal was no longer financially attractive at that price. With Netflix's matching rights expired, WBD and Paramount Skydance executed a definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger. Paramount agreed to acquire all outstanding shares of WBD for $31.00 per share in cash, representing a total enterprise value of approximately $110.9 billion. The transaction covers the entire WBD enterprise and does not require completion of the Discovery Global separation. The Merger Agreement sets a deal end date of March 4, 2027, subject to extension under certain conditions. Paramount committed to pay a reverse termination fee of $5.8 billion if the transaction fails to close for regulatory reasons. WBD's termination of its Netflix merger agreement triggered the $2.8 billion termination fee payable to Netflix, which Paramount agreed to fund."
context: "Netflix's decision not to match is a clear-eyed capital allocation call. At $27.75, Netflix was acquiring the Streaming and Studios business -- HBO, WB Studios, and the content library -- at a price its board and management judged to be financially defensible. At $31.00, for the entire enterprise including the Global Networks linear business, the math no longer worked. Matching Paramount would have required Netflix to either accept a diluted return on the studio assets or overvalue the linear business it had no strategic use for.\n\nThe co-CEOs' use of the phrase 'declining to match' is precise and deliberate. It avoids framing the decision as a defeat while making the financial logic clear: this is not a deal Netflix lost; it is a deal Netflix chose not to win at that price. That framing matters for investor communications and for Netflix's own narrative around capital discipline.\n\nFor WBD shareholders, the economics shifted materially over the course of this process. The December 2025 Netflix offer implied $27.75 in cash per share plus Discovery Global shares. The final outcome delivers $31.00 in cash for the entire company, with no separation required. That is a cleaner, higher-value outcome on the equity side, assuming the deal closes.\n\nThe $2.8 billion Netflix termination fee is notable. Paramount agreed to fund it, which means Paramount effectively absorbed a $2.8 billion cost as the price of winning the bidding war. That sum flows from WBD to Netflix and reflects the breakup fee structure negotiated into the original merger agreement. From a corporate actions standpoint, the definitive Paramount agreement now governs the transaction path forward: tender mechanics, closing conditions, regulatory milestones, and the debt management process that runs concurrently."
