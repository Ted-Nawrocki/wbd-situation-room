## Cast of characters

These transactions are executed by people, not abstractions. Understanding who is making decisions, and from what seat, matters as much as understanding the documents themselves.

### Warner Bros. Discovery

**David Zaslav**  
President and Chief Executive Officer  
The central figure in this story. Oversaw the separation strategy, balance sheet decisions, and the board's navigation of a contested bidding process that ultimately produced a better outcome for shareholders than the original Netflix transaction.

**Gunnar Wiedenfels**  
Chief Financial Officer  
Architect of the June 2025 <span class="glossary-term" data-term="liability-management">liability management</span> and separation framework. Owns the mechanics that made subsequent bids structurally possible -- and now oversees the debt management process running concurrently with regulatory approval.

**Samuel A. Di Piazza, Jr.**  
Chair of the Board of Directors  
Led the board through the Superior Proposal determination process, the Netflix waiver negotiation, and the transition from contested situation to signed deal. His public communications during the contested period set the tone for the board's fiduciary posture.

---

### Netflix

**Ted Sarandos**  
Co-Chief Executive Officer  
Represented the content and studio-driven rationale behind Netflix's original offer. Announced the decision to decline to match Paramount's $31.00/share bid on February 27, 2026.

**Greg Peters**  
Co-Chief Executive Officer  
Jointly announced the withdrawal with Sarandos. Netflix's framing of the decision as a capital discipline call -- "declining to match" rather than losing the bid -- was precise and deliberate.

**Spencer Neumann**  
Chief Financial Officer  
Responsible for the financing analysis that concluded the deal was no longer financially attractive at $31.00 for the whole company.

**David Ellison**

This is one of those rare corporate actions stories that plays like prestige TV: iconic studios, streaming giants, a leveraged capital structure, and a contested process where deadlines and documentation were part of the weaponry.

This page tells the story in episodes.  
**Just the facts** are kept clean.  
**Ted's Takes** are where I connect the dots, professionally, and with a little knowing wit. Because if Hollywood taught us anything, it's that the best dramas are the ones where the "supporting characters" -- financing, covenants, consents, sequencing -- quietly run the show.

---

## Episode 1: The origin story

### Just the facts
- In May 2021, AT&T announced a transaction to separate WarnerMedia and combine it with Discovery.
- The transaction closed in April 2022, creating Warner Bros. Discovery as a publicly traded company.

### Ted's Takes
This is the pilot episode where the audience thinks the stars are the characters on screen. In reality, the balance sheet is cast in a lead role. From inception, WBD's strategic range of motion was going to be shaped by leverage, integration, and the long shadow of linear TV economics.

---

## Episode 2: Living with the balance sheet

### Just the facts
- After formation, WBD emphasized cost reduction, free cash flow, and deleveraging.
- The business operated against two opposing backdrops: structural pressure in linear networks and relentless competition in streaming.

### Ted's Takes
This is the season that feels slow, but it's where the plot locks in. When a company is constrained, the "options" that survive are the ones the capital structure allows. Over time, that creates a quiet inevitability: you either re-architect the enterprise, or the market does it for you.

---

## Episode 3: June 2025 -- the refinancing that paved the runway

### Just the facts
- In June 2025, WBD announced plans to separate into two publicly traded companies: **Streaming & Studios** and **Global Networks**.
- In connection with that plan, WBD pursued a broad debt refinancing / <span class="glossary-term" data-term="liability-management">liability management</span> effort (including tender offers and consents) supported by committed bridge financing, as disclosed in public filings.

### Ted's Takes
If you want the hinge moment for this entire saga, it isn't the hostile bid headline. It's June 2025.

Why? Because contested outcomes require *mechanical* feasibility, not just strategic logic.

The June 2025 execution did the hard, unglamorous work that makes later "big" moves possible:

1) **It clarified the asset boundary.**  
   Two companies: one built around **cash-generating, slower-growth networks**, one built around **streaming and studios**.

2) **It aligned capital structure with business reality.**  
   This is portfolio strategy in plain clothes. The legacy cash engine can carry leverage. The growth business needs oxygen.

3) **It reduced the number of deal-killers.**  
   Liability management is not cosmetics. It's the plumbing that makes a spin, a sale, or a contested process executable without the whole thing seizing up in covenants, maturities, and consent thresholds.

Bottom line: **June 2025 paved the runway.**  
Netflix and Paramount didn't invent the runway. They showed up once the lights were on.

---

## Episode 4: The bidders enter

### Just the facts
- Netflix announced a definitive transaction framework to acquire Warner Bros. (Streaming & Studios) **following the separation**, using a **cash + stock** structure initially reported at **$27.75/share** (split: **$23.25 cash + approximately $4.50 stock**).
- Paramount launched an **all-cash <span class="glossary-term" data-term="tender-offer">tender offer</span>** to acquire WBD for **$30.00/share**.

### Ted's Takes
Now the story switches genres from "restructuring" to "contested process."

Paramount's pitch is straightforward: **cash for everything**. No spin choreography required.

Netflix's pitch is more surgical: buy the growth engine, but only after the company completes the separation. In corporate actions terms, this is the difference between "Tender your shares, get paid" and "First we reorganize the stage, then we film the finale."

Both are credible. Both are complex in different ways. And both place enormous weight on execution.

---

## Episode 5: Delaware Court and the structural critique

### Just the facts
- Paramount filed suit in Delaware Chancery Court on January 13, 2026, alleging breach of fiduciary duty and seeking disclosure of WBD's financial analysis behind the Netflix recommendation.
- Paramount filed a motion for expedited proceedings, citing the January 21, 2026 tender deadline.
- On January 15, 2026, Vice Chancellor Morgan T. Zurn denied the motion to expedite, ruling that Paramount failed to demonstrate "cognizable irreparable harm."
- Paramount announced plans to nominate directors for WBD's 2026 annual meeting, initiating a proxy contest.

### Ted's Takes
This is where the plot shifts from "competing offers" to "parallel campaigns." Paramount is operating on multiple fronts: litigation for disclosure, a tender offer for shareholder action, and a proxy contest for board control.

The Delaware ruling matters, but not for the reasons headlines suggest. The court did not rule on the merits. It ruled on procedure. The litigation path and the tender path had decoupled. Paramount had to rely on shareholder persuasion, not court-ordered disclosure, to shift momentum.

Meanwhile, the proxy contest introduced a longer game -- and signaled Paramount was prepared to fight this over quarters, not weeks.

---

## Episode 6: Netflix eliminates the structural argument

### Just the facts
- On January 20, 2026, Netflix and WBD announced an amendment to their merger agreement, converting the transaction to all-cash consideration.
- The amended offer remains $27.75 per share, eliminating the stock component from the original structure.
- WBD stockholders would receive $27.75 in cash plus shares of Discovery Global following separation.
- WBD filed its preliminary proxy statement on January 20, 2026.
- Companies targeted shareholder vote by April 2026, accelerating from the prior late spring/summer target.

### Ted's Takes
This is a tactical recalibration, not a price increase. Netflix identified the most persuasive element of Paramount's argument -- the structural uncertainty of a cash-and-stock deal -- and removed it from the board.

It forced the comparison back to price. Is $2.25 per share enough of a premium to justify Paramount's whole-company acquisition versus Netflix's targeted acquisition of the growth assets?

In the end, it wasn't.

One final observation: all-cash structures simplify corporate actions execution. There are no exchange ratios to administer, no fractional shares to resolve, and no post-closing adjustments. Netflix moved from complex to clean. But Paramount had one more move left.

---

## Episode 7: The Superior Proposal

### Just the facts
- On February 10, 2026, Paramount filed an 8-K announcing a revised offer letter and an extension of its tender offer.
- WBD's board, through Chairman Di Piazza, issued a unanimous rejection, stating that Paramount's offer remained "inferior to our merger agreement with Netflix across multiple key areas" and provided "insufficient value."
- On February 26, 2026, after receiving a contractual waiver from Netflix, WBD's board reopened negotiations with Paramount.
- Following that review, the board unanimously determined that Paramount's revised all-cash offer of $31.00 per share constituted a "Superior Proposal" under the terms of the Netflix merger agreement.
- The Superior Proposal determination triggered Netflix's contractual matching rights period.

### Ted's Takes
Two things happened here that are easy to miss.

First, Netflix gave its permission. The contractual waiver Netflix granted WBD to engage with Paramount was not a passive event or a technicality. Netflix had to actively choose to allow the board to have this conversation. That decision likely reflected Netflix management's own analysis: Paramount was at a price level where Netflix's own return on the studio assets would be impaired if it matched. Better to let the process run and collect the $2.8 billion termination fee than to overpay.

Second, the move from $30.00 to $31.00 per share is what made this happen. Paramount had been at $30.00 since December. The $1.00 increase to $31.00 -- combined with the whole-company scope requiring no separation -- gave the WBD board the basis to conclude the price gap had become a legitimate fiduciary issue. At $30.00, the board could credibly argue Netflix's offer was comparable. At $31.00 for everything, that argument became harder to sustain.

The board's public posture throughout the contested period -- consistent, disciplined, emphasizing execution risk rather than simply defending the Netflix price -- gave them credibility in this moment. When they said the Paramount offer was now superior, the market believed them.

---

## Episode 8: Netflix Exits, Paramount Signs

### Just the facts
- On February 27, 2026, Netflix co-CEOs Ted Sarandos and Greg Peters announced Netflix would not raise its offer, declining to match Paramount's $31.00/share bid.
- WBD and Paramount Skydance signed a definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger on February 27, 2026.
- Paramount agreed to acquire all outstanding WBD shares at $31.00 per share in cash, representing an enterprise value of approximately $110.9 billion.
- The transaction covers the entire WBD enterprise with no separation required.
- The Merger Agreement sets a deal end date of March 4, 2027, subject to extension.
- Paramount's reverse termination fee is $5.8 billion. WBD's termination of the Netflix agreement triggered the $2.8 billion Netflix termination fee, which Paramount agreed to fund.

### Ted's Takes
Netflix's exit language is worth reading carefully. "Declining to match" is precise. Not "we lost." Not "we are withdrawing." They are framing this as a choice, not a defeat, and the framing is defensible. At $31.00 for the whole enterprise, the math had moved outside the range that justified the deal for Netflix shareholders.

For the people who process these transactions, the February 27 signing date is the moment the operational playbook changes. From this point, the relevant documents are the Paramount merger agreement, the Paramount Schedule TO and its amendments, and the debt management filings. The Netflix proxy materials, the 14D-9 amendments rejecting Paramount, the Delaware litigation -- those are now history, not working documents.

The $2.8 billion Netflix termination fee flowing from WBD to Netflix -- funded by Paramount -- is an elegant piece of deal economics. Paramount absorbed a $2.8 billion cost as the price of winning. That sum is not trivial, but it is also not what determined the outcome. The outcome was determined by price and scope.

---

## Episode 9: The Shareholders Speak

### Just the facts
- On April 23, 2026, WBD stockholders voted at a Special Meeting to approve the Paramount Skydance merger.
- Over 1.7 billion votes were cast in favor; approximately 16.3 million votes were cast against.
- Shareholders did not approve the executive compensation package presented at the same meeting.
- The transaction remains subject to regulatory clearances from the DOJ and European regulators.
- Both companies continue to target closing in Q3 2026.
- Paramount noted completed equity and debt syndications and reported progress across regulatory approvals.

### Ted's Takes
1.7 billion to 16.3 million. That is not a vote -- it is a verdict.

When shareholders vote this overwhelmingly, it tells you something important: the institutional investor community had already made up its mind well before the meeting date. The proxy process, the road shows, the analyst calls -- all of it had landed. The result was a formality that confirmed a conclusion investors had already reached.

The failed say-on-pay vote is a footnote, not a signal. Advisory compensation votes in deal contexts frequently reflect residual governance dissatisfaction that has nothing to do with the transaction itself. No institutional investor voted against the merger over the pay package.

The operational transition following this vote is real. With shareholder risk removed, custody systems and operations teams can now move from tracking a contested situation to managing a pending acquisition. The remaining open items are regulatory, and those run on a different clock than equity markets.

## Episode 10: The Debt Management Curtain Call

### Just the facts
- On May 19, 2026, WBD subsidiary issuers Discovery Global Holdings and Discovery Communications launched consent solicitations across 17 series of outstanding notes, covering interest rates from 3.950% to 5.300% and maturities from 2027 to 2052. The consent deadline is May 26, 2026. The consent payment is $2.50 / EUR 2.50 per $1,000 / EUR 1,000 principal amount, funded by Paramount, with payment expected on or about May 29, 2026.
- On the same date, Paramount Skydance launched concurrent but structurally distinct cash tender offers and exchange offers on notes issued by the same WBD subsidiary issuers. The tender offers and exchange offers target entirely different note series. The tender offers cover two short-dated series only -- the 3.755% Senior Notes due 2027 (DGH Issuer, approximately $1.195 billion outstanding) and the 3.950% Senior Notes due 2028 (DCL Issuer, approximately $1.249 billion outstanding) -- for cash consideration at par, capped at $2.4 billion in aggregate. The exchange offers cover thirteen longer-dated series ranging from 2029 to 2052, across USD and euro denominations from both issuers, capped at $12.8 billion in aggregate. Holders who tender in the exchange offers receive newly issued Paramount second-lien secured notes with matching currency, maturities, interest payment dates, and rates. Both sets of offers are structured as any-and-all offers with no proration mechanism. Each series is a separate offer. Both sets expire June 17, 2026. Settlement is expected on or shortly after the acquisition closing date.
- The exchange offers are restricted to eligible holders only: qualified institutional buyers under Rule 144A or non-U.S. persons under Regulation S. Non-QIB domestic holders may not participate in the exchange offers.
- Participation in either offer requires prior delivery of consents in the concurrent WBD consent solicitations. Upon delivering consents, holders receive a temporary identifier (temporary CUSIP or ISIN) for their notes; only notes bearing a temporary identifier are eligible to participate in the applicable offer.
- Paramount has disclosed plans to replace its $49 billion bridge loan with approximately $39.5 billion in first-lien and $12.4 billion in second-lien secured debt. Paramount has committed to ratings agencies to delever to below 3.75x net debt to adjusted EBITDA by 2028 and 3.0x by 2029.
- Paramount has disclosed a target of over $6 billion in combined cost and revenue synergies, with approximately 30% expected in the first year post-close, 70% by year two, and full run-rate by year three.

### Ted's Takes
Three concurrent corporate actions events, all launched on the same day, all linked to each other, all conditional on an acquisition that has not yet closed. This is the supporting cast running the show.

Start with the consent solicitation. On its face, it is a housekeeping exercise: extend a deadline from December 30, 2026 to March 4, 2027. In practice, it is a gating mechanism. Without the consents, the Paramount offers cannot proceed. And without the Paramount offers, the debt structure that Paramount needs in place for the combined company cannot be built. The five-day consent window -- launched May 19, deadline May 26 -- is tight by design. Paramount and WBD need this cleared quickly to maintain momentum on the overall closing timeline.

The structural split between the tender and exchange offers is deliberate and worth understanding precisely. The two short-dated series -- the 2027 and 2028 maturities -- are being taken out for cash. They mature so soon that substituting them with newly issued long-dated Paramount secured notes would create a maturity mismatch that makes no economic sense for either side. Cash retirement is the clean solution: Paramount pays par plus accrued interest, the instruments are cancelled, and the WBD issuers' obligations on those two series are extinguished at closing. The thirteen longer-dated series -- 2029 through 2052 -- are being exchanged for newly issued Paramount second-lien secured notes with matching terms. Those instruments have enough remaining life to make the obligor substitution commercially rational. From a holder's perspective, the exchange is a credit substitution: the obligor changes from a WBD subsidiary to Paramount Skydance, the security package changes from unsecured to second-lien secured, and the credit exposure is now to a substantially more leveraged combined entity.

There is no proration risk. Both sets of offers are structured as any-and-all, and the caps -- $2.4 billion for the tender, $12.8 billion for the exchange -- are sized to accommodate the full outstanding principal of each respective pool. Under full participation, both pools fit within the caps.

The eligibility restriction on the exchange offers is operationally significant and easy to overlook. Only QIBs and non-U.S. persons under Regulation S may participate. Custodians and intermediaries must screen their holder populations before submitting exchange instructions. Non-eligible holders of exchange offer notes who have delivered consents will receive the consent payment, but cannot receive new PSKY notes. Their existing notes remain outstanding WBD subsidiary obligations until maturity or other disposition.

The temporary identifier mechanic creates a trading split that operations teams need to handle carefully. Once a holder delivers consent, their notes are re-identified with a temporary CUSIP or ISIN and trade separately from non-consenting holders' notes. Two distinct trading populations exist for each series between consent delivery and offer expiration. Systems that cannot track both the original and temporary identifiers simultaneously will create reconciliation problems at settlement.

Finally, settlement does not occur at expiration. Both offers settle on or shortly after the acquisition closing, targeted Q3 2026 but subject to DOJ and European regulatory timelines. A holder who tenders on June 16 may wait months for settlement. During that window, tendered notes remain outstanding obligations of the WBD issuers, positions need to be tracked in a tendered-but-unsettled state, and the settlement clock does not start until regulatory clearance is received. That is a non-trivial custody management challenge across what could be a multi-month window.
