*Updated May 2026. The bidding war resolved on February 27, 2026. Shareholder approval followed on April 23. The framework below reflects the current phase: a signed deal awaiting regulatory clearance, with an active debt management exercise running concurrently.*

---

### 1. A signed agreement is not a closed transaction.

The WBD board approved the deal. Shareholders voted 1.7 billion to 16.3 million in favor. Equity and debt syndications are complete. None of that closes the transaction.

The remaining conditions are DOJ antitrust clearance, European regulatory approval, and resolution of the California AG investigation. These run on their own calendars, independent of shareholder sentiment or deal economics. The DOJ head stated publicly in March 2026 that Paramount would receive no fast-track treatment. That is a posture statement, not a blocking signal, but it confirms the review will be thorough.

The $5.8 billion reverse termination fee Paramount committed to reflects the parties' own assessment of regulatory risk. It belongs in any analysis of what this deal actually costs.

---

### 2. June 2025 built the runway.

The decisive shift in this story occurred before either bidder appeared.

In June 2025, WBD executed its separation framework and a related liability management exercise. That work defined which assets could travel together, aligned debt with the business units that could service it, and removed covenant and maturity obstacles that would have made a sale impractical. Netflix and Paramount did not create the opportunity. They showed up after the conditions for a transaction were in place.

---

### 3. Execution is the constraint. Capital was never the differentiator.

Both bidders could access financing. The real constraints were procedural: sequencing of steps, satisfaction of conditions, regulatory clearance, consent thresholds, operational readiness across intermediaries.

In the closing phase, this applies with more force. The path from signed agreement to closed transaction runs through regulatory processes, debt management operations, and custody mechanics that neither party controls. Speed and quality of execution in each area will determine whether the Q3 2026 target holds.

---

### 4. Think in regulatory paths, not just a binary outcome.

DOJ review can produce unconditional clearance, clearance with structural remedies, clearance with behavioral conditions, extended second-request review, or litigation. Each has different implications for timing, deal economics, and the combined company's starting position. The reverse termination fee calibrates the financial stakes of the scenario where close does not happen.

European regulators add a second track with its own timeline. Both need to be monitored independently.

---

### 5. The debt management exercise is the active operational story.

For corporate actions practitioners, the consent solicitations launched May 19 and expiring May 26 are the most time-sensitive current item. Paramount's tender and exchange offers expire June 17. All three operations are conditioned on the acquisition closing, linked through a consent sequencing dependency, and subject to eligibility restrictions that require active screening. Settlement follows regulatory close, not the offer expiration dates.

---

### 6. Politics provides context, not outcomes.

Media assets carry cultural and public-interest weight that antitrust economics does not fully capture. The DOJ posture statement, the California AG investigation, and broad political attention on media consolidation all shape the process environment. Practitioners should monitor that context without assuming it determines legal outcomes.

---

### 7. This is a corporate actions story wearing an M&A headline.

The decisive moments in this situation have occurred around record dates, eligibility determinations, tender mechanics, consent thresholds, temporary identifiers, and settlement timing tied to regulatory events. Strategy set the direction. Corporate actions mechanics determine whether the deal lands.

---

### Closing thought

A signed deal at $110.9 billion enterprise value, $49 billion in bridge financing to refinance, 17 series of notes in active consent solicitations, concurrent tender and exchange offers expiring June 17, and a DOJ review with no confirmed timeline is not a routine situation.

Read the primary documents. Track the deadlines. Understand the sequencing. Do not confuse a signed agreement with a closed transaction.
