**As of May 21, 2026**

The bidding war is resolved. The agreement is signed. Shareholders have approved.

What remains is where the operational work actually lives.

---

## 1. The closing phase has its own operational structure

The contest phase tested systems' ability to track competing offers, handle conditional eligibility, and manage multiple simultaneous transaction paths. That phase is over.

The closing phase tests something different: managing a multi-instrument liability management exercise in parallel with a regulatory approval process that neither party controls.

Three concurrent operations launched on May 19, 2026: WBD consent solicitations across 17 note series, with a deadline of May 26; Paramount cash tender offers on two short-dated series, expiring June 17; and Paramount exchange offers on thirteen longer-dated series, also expiring June 17. All three are conditioned on the acquisition closing. Settlement of the tender and exchange offers follows the closing date, targeted Q3 2026 but subject to DOJ and European timelines. Positions must be managed in an open, unsettled state for a window that could extend months beyond June 17.

---

## 2. The tender and exchange offers target different instruments

The two tender offer series (3.755% due 2027 and 3.950% due 2028) are retired for cash. Holders who tender exit the WBD capital structure at settlement. The WBD subsidiary issuers' obligations on those two series are extinguished at close.

The thirteen exchange offer series (2029 through 2052, USD and euro) are exchanged for newly issued Paramount Skydance second-lien secured notes with matching terms. The obligor changes from a WBD subsidiary to Paramount Skydance. The security package changes from unsecured senior to second-lien secured. The credit exposure is now to a combined entity with considerably more leverage. The coupon is the same. The credit is not.

Operations systems, booking entries, and client communications need to reflect that distinction from exchange instruction through to settlement.

---

## 3. Consent delivery gates everything else

Participation in either Paramount offer requires prior delivery of consents in the WBD consent solicitations. Missing the May 26 deadline gates a holder out of both offers for that series entirely. That is an irreversible consequence of a missed processing deadline.

Upon consent delivery, notes are re-identified with a temporary CUSIP or ISIN. Those notes trade separately from non-consenting positions in the same series, creating two populations that must be tracked simultaneously and linked correctly through to settlement.

---

## 4. The exchange offers carry a hard eligibility restriction

The Paramount exchange offers are available only to qualified institutional buyers under Rule 144A or non-U.S. persons under Regulation S. The offers are not registered. Non-QIB domestic holders are ineligible regardless of consent delivery.

Custodians and intermediaries must screen holder populations before submitting exchange instructions. Non-eligible holders of exchange offer series who deliver consent receive the $2.50 payment but hold WBD subsidiary obligations after close. Systems must reflect the correct obligor, identifier, and governing documentation for those positions from closing forward.

---

## 5. Settlement does not occur at offer expiration

Both offers expire June 17, 2026. Settlement occurs on or shortly after acquisition close, targeted Q3 2026. Tendered and exchanged notes remain outstanding WBD subsidiary obligations until close. Interest continues to accrue and is paid through the settlement date. The interest bridge to the new PSKY notes is clean in the transaction documents. Replicating it cleanly in operations systems across potentially large numbers of individual position records requires preparation before June 17, not after.

---

## 6. Scale amplifies every failure mode

Seventeen consent series. Thirteen exchange series. Two tender series. Aggregate principal of approximately $16.6 billion across USD and euro denominations, two issuers, two separate indentures, distributed across the global custodian chain.

Scale does not change what the failure modes are. It changes how quickly they become material.

---

## 7. Regulatory conditionality cannot be managed away

Every element of the debt management exercise is conditioned on the acquisition closing. Operations teams can be perfectly prepared for June 17 and still face a months-long unsettled position state if regulatory clearance is delayed.

That requires workflows designed around two scenarios simultaneously: a Q3 2026 close and a delayed close. The $5.8 billion reverse termination fee protects against the scenario where close does not happen at all. Operations teams should understand the fallback mechanics in the WBD indentures and Paramount offer documents for that scenario, even if they hope never to apply them.

---

## 8. Corporate actions expertise is core risk management here

Whether the Paramount-WBD combination delivers value will depend in part on whether the transaction closes cleanly, on time, and without operational failures that create legal or financial exposure for the parties and intermediaries in the custody chain.

The consent deadline is May 26. The offer expiration is June 17. Settlement follows regulatory close. Each of those dates is a point at which operational readiness is tested against real consequences.

---

Contested transactions of this scale do not fail because the strategy was unclear. They fail because execution breaks under complexity. The WBD situation has demonstrated this in both phases: structural complexity during the bidding war, operational complexity during the closing. The professionals who understand both phases are the ones whose clients get through this cleanly.
