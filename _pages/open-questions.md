## Active open questions

These are the questions that remain genuinely unanswered as of May 21, 2026 -- the ones that will determine whether and how the transaction closes, and what the combined company looks like on the other side.

---

### I. Regulatory approval

**1. Will the DOJ approve the transaction, and on what timeline?**

The head of the DOJ Antitrust Division stated publicly in March 2026 that Paramount will not receive fast-track treatment. That establishes posture, not outcome. The substantive questions are: what theory of harm drives the review, what remedies -- if any -- are required, and does the review conclude before the March 4, 2027 Merger Agreement end date?

**2. What conditions, if any, will the DOJ attach to clearance?**

Structural remedies -- divestitures of specific assets or businesses -- would change the combined company's operating profile and could affect the synergy targets Paramount has disclosed. Behavioral remedies -- conduct commitments around content licensing or distribution -- could constrain the combined entity's commercial flexibility for years after close. Understanding the range of plausible conditions is important context for anyone evaluating the combined company's long-term economics.

**3. What is the European regulatory timeline, and does it run parallel to or after DOJ?**

The EU review process runs on its own calendar. Does the combined entity's European footprint -- free-to-air channels, streaming, content distribution -- create concentration concerns that European regulators will review independently of the DOJ? Does a phase two investigation become a realistic possibility?

**4. What does the California AG investigation produce?**

A formal challenge from California would introduce a parallel legal track. Absence of public developments is not the same as resolution. This remains a disclosed risk factor with no confirmed outcome.

**5. Does regulatory review push the close beyond Q3 2026?**

The Merger Agreement end date is March 4, 2027. If close does not occur by that date, either party may terminate the agreement subject to conditions. The $5.8 billion reverse termination fee is the economic consequence of a regulatory failure to close. How close is the Q3 2026 target to the actual regulatory timeline?

### II. Debt management operations

**6. Do the WBD consent solicitations achieve required majorities across all 17 series?**

The consent deadline is May 26, 2026. The required threshold is a majority in aggregate principal amount per indenture. Are there individual series where holder concentration or mandate constraints create uncertainty about achieving the required threshold?

**7. What is the participation rate in Paramount's exchange offers?**

Notes not exchanged remain outstanding WBD subsidiary obligations after close. High participation simplifies the combined company's post-close capital structure. Lower participation leaves a legacy tail of WBD subsidiary obligations that the combined entity must manage alongside its new Paramount-issued instruments. The QIB eligibility restriction sets a ceiling on participation that is independent of holder sentiment.

**8. If the acquisition does not close, what are the fallback mechanics?**

The consent solicitation amendments and the Paramount offers are all conditioned on the acquisition closing. If the Merger Agreement is terminated, the WBD indenture amendments specify fallback terms -- the Required Exchange Transaction deadline reverts to either December 30, 2026 or 90 days after termination, whichever is later. Have holders and their advisors reviewed those fallback provisions carefully? The Paramount offers also terminate without settlement in a no-close scenario.

**9. How does the temporary identifier mechanic affect secondary market trading between now and June 17?**

Consenting holders receive a temporary CUSIP or ISIN for their notes, which then trade separately from non-consenting holders' notes. Does this create bid-ask spread disruption or liquidity fragmentation in the affected series during the six-week window between consent delivery and offer expiration?

### III. Post-close capital structure and execution

**10. How does Paramount's permanent debt financing execute against current market conditions?**

The $49 billion bridge loan needs to be replaced with approximately $39.5 billion first-lien and $12.4 billion second-lien secured debt. Market conditions, credit spreads, and ratings agency assessments at the time of execution will determine the actual cost. If conditions are unfavorable at closing, does Paramount have flexibility on timing or structure?

**11. Are the deleveraging commitments achievable on the disclosed timeline?**

Paramount has committed to ratings agencies to reach below 3.75x net debt to adjusted EBITDA by 2028 and 3.0x by 2029. That requires meaningful free cash flow generation from the combined entity. What are the downside scenarios -- a weaker advertising market, content underperformance, integration delays -- and how much headroom exists in the plan?

**12. Will the year-one synergy delivery meet the 30% target?**

Paramount disclosed to eligible holders a target of over $6 billion in total synergies with approximately 30% realized in year one. Integration plans of this complexity -- two legacy media companies with overlapping studios, networks, streaming platforms, and international operations -- rarely execute exactly as modeled. Where is the integration plan most exposed to slippage?

**13. What does the combined company's content strategy look like post-close?**

Two studios, two streaming platforms, and a collection of legacy cable networks create decisions that go beyond cost cutting. How does the combined entity manage IP, theatrical release strategy, and streaming content allocation without cannibalizing its own assets or confusing audiences?

---

### IV. The question behind the questions

**14. At what price does regulatory risk become a deal risk?**

The $5.8 billion reverse termination fee calibrates the financial stakes of a regulatory failure. But the reputational, operational, and strategic costs of a protracted regulatory battle -- or a failed close -- extend well beyond that fee. How does each party's calculus on those broader costs shape how aggressively they negotiate with regulators over potential remedies?

**15. What story is the combined company telling about the future of media, and is it executable?**

The strategic rationale for the combination -- scale in content, streaming, and distribution -- requires execution in a market that is still in transition. Whether the combined Paramount-WBD-Skydance entity actually delivers on that thesis will be the question that defines this deal's legacy, not the price paid or the bidding war won.

## Resolved questions (archived for reference)

The following questions drove the analysis during the bidding war period (December 2025 through February 2026). They are preserved here for reference and for readers who want to understand how the situation evolved.

**Board process and fiduciary posture** -- Resolved February 26-27, 2026. The WBD board declared Paramount's $31.00/share offer a Superior Proposal after receiving a contractual waiver from Netflix. Netflix declined to match. WBD signed with Paramount.

**Netflix vs. Paramount transaction architecture** -- Resolved. Netflix's merger agreement was terminated on February 27, 2026. The Discovery Global separation is no longer required. The transaction is a single-step all-cash acquisition of the entire WBD enterprise.

**Shareholder vote** -- Resolved April 23, 2026. WBD shareholders approved the Paramount merger overwhelmingly: over 1.7 billion votes in favor against approximately 16.3 million against.

**Delaware Chancery Court litigation** -- Resolved. Paramount's disclosure lawsuit and motion for expedited proceedings were effectively superseded by the Superior Proposal determination and the execution of the definitive agreement. The proxy contest was withdrawn.

**Proxy contest** -- Resolved. Paramount's plan to nominate directors for WBD's annual meeting was withdrawn following the execution of the definitive merger agreement.

**Netflix financing structure and all-cash amendment** -- Historical. Netflix converted its offer to all-cash on January 20, 2026. Netflix withdrew on February 27, 2026. These events are documented in the timeline.
